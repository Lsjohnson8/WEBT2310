Our Conservation Mission

Shark conservation is critical for maintaining the balance of marine ecosystems. Sharks are apex predators, meaning they play a vital role in keeping other marine species in check. This regulation ensures that populations of prey species don't overgrow, which could lead to the collapse of ecosystems and affect the biodiversity of the oceans. Without sharks, ecosystems can become unbalanced, leading to overpopulation of certain species, depletion of resources, and even the decline of species essential to marine life.

The Culprit

Water pollution has far-reaching consequences for both marine life and human populations. Pollutants like plastic, chemicals, and agricultural runoff degrade habitats, contaminate species like sharks, and disrupt food chains by killing off smaller marine organisms and plants. This leads to cascading effects, with species dependent on clean water and healthy reefs, such as fish and sharks, becoming especially vulnerable. Pollutants like heavy metals, plastics, and chemicals also result in biodiversity loss, harming coral reefs and other crucial ecosystems. These impacts extend to humans, as toxins from polluted waters enter the food chain through contaminated seafood, affecting health and livelihoods, particularly in coastal areas. Additionally, water pollution contributes to climate change through carbon dioxide emissions and nutrient runoff, leading to ocean warming and acidification, which further threatens marine life and disrupts weather patterns and sea levels globally.

Florida Shark Conservation

Florida has several key organizations dedicated to shark conservation. They focus on research, education, and policy change to protect shark populations and marine ecosystems.

  • Shark Research and Conservation Program (SRC) at the University of Miami: They conduct cutting-edge shark research and offer educational programs to engage the public in shark conservation efforts.
  • The Guy Harvey Ocean Foundation: This foundation supports marine conservation efforts, including shark research and habitat protection.
  • Shark Allies: This group focuses on advocacy, awareness, and policy work to stop shark finning and overfishing, particularly around U.S. waters.
  • Fins Attached: They work on global shark conservation efforts, with strong ties to Florida. Their mission is to promote the preservation of marine ecosystems through research, awareness, and advocacy for shark protection.

These organizations are at the forefront of both local and global efforts to protect sharks and ensure that marine ecosystems remain healthy.

Meet The Sharks!

Reef Shark

Reef sharks are a group of medium-sized sharks commonly found in tropical and subtropical waters around coral reefs, with species like the blacktip reef shark and Caribbean reef shark being the most well-known. They have sleek, streamlined bodies that help them navigate shallow waters, with distinctive markings like the black-tipped fins of the blacktip reef shark or the solid gray color of the Caribbean reef shark. Reef sharks typically range in size from 4.5 to 9.8 feet, depending on the species, and are known for their agility and speed, especially in the shallow waters they inhabit, usually between 1.6 and 165 feet deep. These sharks are generally cautious around humans and are more active during the day, patrolling coral reefs and drop-offs for prey. As carnivores, their diet consists mainly of smaller fish, crustaceans, octopuses, and squid, and they are opportunistic feeders, using their speed to hunt in the early morning or late afternoon when prey is most active near the reef.

Tiger Shark

The tiger shark (Galeocerdo cuvier) is a large, powerful predator easily recognized by its distinctive dark vertical stripes, especially prominent on juveniles, which fade as they age. These sharks have a broad, flat head and blunt snout, with strong jaws filled with serrated teeth capable of cutting through a wide variety of prey. Tiger sharks are among the largest shark species, typically growing between 10 and 14 feet long, with some reaching lengths of over 16 feet and weighing up to 1,400 pounds. They are solitary hunters, primarily nocturnal, and known for their curiosity and adaptability. Tiger sharks are found in tropical and subtropical waters worldwide, preferring coastal areas and depths ranging from shallow waters to around 900 feet, though they frequently swim closer to the surface. Silky Shark

The silky shark (Carcharhinus falciformis) is a sleek, slender species named for its smooth skin and streamlined body, which gives it an elegant appearance. It is typically gray or bronze-colored, with a white underside and long, pointed fins that enhance its agility in the water. Silky sharks usually grow to about 8 to 10 feet in length and can weigh up to 400 pounds. Known for their schooling behavior, especially when young, they are fast, active swimmers that often patrol open waters. These sharks prefer warmer, tropical, and subtropical waters and are commonly found at depths between 60 to 1,600 feet, though they often hunt near the surface. As opportunistic predators, silky sharks primarily feed on bony fish, squid, and octopuses, but they are also known to scavenge. Their adaptability and speed make them effective hunters in pelagic (open ocean) environments, where they play a crucial role in maintaining the balance of marine ecosystems.
